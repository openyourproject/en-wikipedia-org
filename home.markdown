## Today's featured article

|
|

[![An 1878 photograph of Melville Island by Scheuer Notman][19]][20]

**[Melville Island][21]** is a small peninsula in [Nova Scotia][22], Canada, 
located in the [Northwest Arm][23] of [Halifax Harbour][24], west of [Deadman's 
Island][25]. It is part of the [Halifax Regional Municipality][26]. The land 
is rocky, with thin, acidic soil, but supports a limited woodland habitat. 
The site was discovered by Europeans in the 1600s, though it was likely earlier 
explored by [aboriginals][27]. It was initially used for storehouses before 
being purchased by the British, who built a prisoner-of-war camp to hold captives 
from the [Napoleonic Wars][28] and later the [War of 1812][29]. The burial 
ground for the prisoners was on the adjacent Deadman's Island. Melville Island 
was used as a receiving depot for slaves escaping the United States, then as 
a quarantine hospital for immigrants arriving from Europe (particularly Ireland). 
It briefly served as a recruitment centre for the [British Foreign Legion][30] 
during the [Crimean War][31] and was then sold to the British for use as a 
military prison. The land was granted to the Canadian government in 1907, which 
used it to detain German and Austro-Hungarian nationals during the First World 
War. During the Second World War, prisoners were sent to [McNabs Island][32] 
instead, and ammunition depots were kept on Melville Island. The peninsula 
now houses the clubhouse and marina of the [Armdale Yacht Club][33]. Melville 
Island has been the subject of a number of cultural works, most of which concern 
its use as a prison. (**[more...][21]**)

## Today's featured picture

|

|[![Mallard ducks][133]][134]|

A pair of **[Mallards][135]**, an easily recognized species of [dabbling duck][136] 
that is found throughout the [temperate][137] and [subtropical][138] Americas, 
Europe, Asia, and North Africa, as well as Australia and New Zealand. The male 
birds have a bright green head (during breeding season) and are grey on wings 
and belly, while the females are brown all over. Both sexes have blue [speculum 
feathers][139]. Mallards live in [wetlands][140], eat water plants and small 
animals, and are gregarious. This species is the ancestor of almost all of 
the breeds of [domestic ducks][141].
